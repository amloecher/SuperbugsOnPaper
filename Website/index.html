Behind the Scenes

How does the test work? What are its components? What is the science behind each of them?

Carbapenem resistant enterobacteria (CRE) are a class of "superbugs", bacteria that developed resistance to antibiotics that make infections extremely hard to treat - diagnosing these infections is a major challenge.

The device has three different modules:
(1) First the blood is filtered to separate cells and other substances that could interfere with the test.
(2) Next the bacterial DNA associated with CRE infections is amplified into millions of copies.
(3) Finally these copies are bound to a test line and detected with a dye.

What is CRE?

Carbapenem-Resistant Enterobacteriaceae (CRE) are part of the Enterobacteriaceae family, which mainly encompasses bacteria - another well-known bacteria belonging to this family is E. coli. These bacteria can cause severe infections including pneumonia, bloodsteam infections and meningitis.

The reason why CRE are dangerous and have a great potential to spread is because they are resistant to carbapenem antibiotics, which are generally considered as the last line of defense to treat resistant bacterial infections.

Our test will detect the KPC-1, NDM-1 and OXA-48 gene variants of CRE, which are prevalent in South East Asia. By identifying the strain and determining the progress of the infection, patients in low-resource health care settings can have a faster and more effective treatment plan.

You can find out more about CRE and additional facts here.

Device overview

Our solution is a nucleic acid lateral flow test. Using nucleic acids enabled us to test for multiple strains through the process of multiplexing. The test has three test lines, each corresponding with a different strain.

Blood filtration

Whole blood is mainly made up of red blood cells, platelets, white blood cells and plasma. The blood plasma contains genetic information, which is essential for our test to function. The other cell contents can interfere with the test results - hemoglobin from red blood cells can make the plasma red, which makes it difficult for clear detection and why filtering is necessary.

The gold standard today is to use centrifugation or sedimentation. However, these methods require extra equipment, a large blood volume, skilled workers and are time-consuming. With point of care tests, the aim is to reduce the duration of the test, the volume of blood needed and to introduce the possibility of anyone being able to use it - no extra skills needed!

Our solution is to use depth filtration and to test bound glass fiber and cotton linter. As a result of the filtration, large blood cells are mostly trapped on the surface, smaller particles such as white blood cells are caught in the glass/cotton linter fibers, but the smaller plasma particles are allowed through to the next steps of the test.

DNA amplification

DNA amplification is essentially the production of multiple copies of a particular sequence of DNA. Amplification of the DNA is necessary to determine if the DNA associated with CRE is present. The quantity of DNA in filtered blood is initially low, so it needs to be increased so an accurate read-out is obtained. It is usually performed using polymerase chain reaction (PCR), which requires additional equipment, temperatures up to 98°C and is time-consuming.

An alternative method is recombinase polymerase amplification (RPA). It takes ~20 minutes, a lower temperature of 37°C is required for the reaction and no additional equipment is needed.

Next we designed primers specific to the gene variants of CRE - this ensures that only the target sequences are amplified. RPA primers should be 30-35 base pairs in length, whereas PCR primers are usually 20 base pairs. Additionally, a GC content of 40-60% is necessary for RPA, whereas for PCR, a GC content of 55% is optimal.

We used the TwistAmp® nfo kit for amplification. A more detailed description of the kit can be found here.

Colorimetric detection

Colorimetric detection allows for quantitative data to be interpreted and analyzed as well as reducing the volume of human interpretation errors. The results are uploaded to global monitoring feature (while still maintaining confidnentiality), which is a great benefit as only 3 out of 11 countries in South East Asia have monitoring programmes for these infections.

Anyone with access to WiFi or broadband can view this feature.

Results and global monitoring

Our motivation for a global database is to have an easy visualization of the affected areas and to quickly ascertain the threat level in these areas.

Due to the complexity of molecular testing, most clinicians don't understand the clinical significance of detecting specific resistance genes or discordance between phenotypic and genotypic tests. Thus there is a need to integrate results and communication between clinicians and clinical decision support systems to optimize patient outcomes.
