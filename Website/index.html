Behind the Scenes

How does the test work? What are its components? What is the science behind each of them?

Carbapenem resistant enterobacteria (CRE) are a class of "superbugs", bacteria that developed resistance to antibiotics that make infections extremely hard to treat. Diagnosing these infections is a major challenge.
The device has three different modules: first the blood is filtered to separate cells and other substances that could interfere with the test; then bacterial DNA associated with CRE infections is amplified into millions of copies; finally these copies are bound to a test line and detected with a dye.

What is CRE?

Carbapenem-Resistant Enterobacteriaceae (CRE) are part of the Enterobacteriaceae family, which mainly encompasses bacteria - another well-known bacteria belonging to this family is E. coli. These bacteria can cause severe infections including pneumonia, bloodsteam infections and meningitis.

The reason why CRE are dangerous and have a great potential to spread is because they are resistant to carbapenem antibiotics, which are generally considered as the last line of defense to treat resistant bacterial infections.

Our test will detect the KPC-1, NDM-1 and OXA-48 gene variants of CRE, which are prevalent in South East Asia. By identifying the strain and determining the progress of the infection, patients in low-resource health care settings can have a faster and more effective treatment plan.
